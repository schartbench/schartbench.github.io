Abstract

Chart question answering (CQA) has become a critical multimodal task for evaluating the reasoning capabilities of vision-language models. While early approaches have shown promising performance by focusing on visual features or leveraging large-scale pre-training, most existing evaluations rely on fixed output formats, which fail to capture real-world, subjective tasks. In this paper, we introduce SChartBench, the first benchmark designed for subjective CQA in practical settings. SChartBench encompasses seven task categories: Chart Conversion, Chart OCR Recognition, Suggestions, Chart Category Analysis, Chart Summary, Chart Assistance, and Information Positioning. It features multilingual chart data and diverse chart formats. We conduct extensive evaluations on SChartBench with 14 mainstream multimodal models across two languages and seven chart types. We compare ChartLLM with three common CQA paradigms: instruction-following, OCR-enhanced, and chain-of-thought. The results have shown that ChartLLM-based methods significantly outperform these paradigms, highlighting the importance of flexible chart understanding for real-world CQA. These findings also point to new directions for developing more robust subjective chart reasoning in future research.

Challenges in Existing CQA Benchmarks

To better understand the strengths and weaknesses of MLLMs in CQA tasks, several evaluation benchmarks have been proposed. Human evaluations are often highly variable and unstable due to individual and cultural differences. Therefore, most benchmarks focus on automatic metrics, such as ChartQA, OpenCQA, Chart-to-Text, and LineCap. These benchmarks mainly emphasize tasks such as generating statistical summaries or titles for scientific charts, relying on fixed output formats that can be evaluated using objective metrics. While they are effective for evaluation, they may not fully capture the actual challenges faced by professionals, especially those involving complex reasoning and decision making using real-world chart data. The figure below highlights the three major challenges in existing benchmarks: multilingual charts, diverse formats, and subjective answers. Models need to handle visual understanding and logical reasoning simultaneously. They must first recognize visual elements (such as color, structure, and spatial relationships) to extract meaningful information. Then, they need to analyze the logical connections between elements and answer complex queries, such as performing calculations, identifying trends, and finding relationships in data. In addition, the wide variety of chart types in the real world (e.g., bar charts, line charts, scatter plots) places higher requirements on the model's generalization ability and performing well on new and unseen formats.

Data Processing and Dataset Construction

Stage I: Chart Data Collection: Chart data is collected from diverse open sources, including public datasets, GitHub repositories, and Overleaf projects, ensuring compliance with open licenses (e.g., CC BY 4.0, MIT) as per OpenCQA and ChartQA standards. The dataset, comprising over 1,200 charts in English and Chinese, covers a range of types such as Pie Charts, Stacked Bar Charts, Scatter Plots, Common and Grouped Bar Charts, as well as Simple and Complex Line Charts. Following collection, a manual filtering process ensures charts meet subjective reasoning requirements by excluding those with deficiencies (e.g., missing labels, unreadable fonts). This curation establishes a high-quality dataset foundation.
Stage II: Question-Answer Pair Generation: To develop a benchmark for subjective CQA tasks, GPT-4o generates question-answer pairs across seven subjective reasoning categories. Prompts and few-shot examples guide the model to produce diverse, context-specific queries and responses, tailored to tasks such as trend summarization, actionable suggestions, and chart structure analysis. Task-specific questions are paired with answers that align with chart context and objectives, ensuring logical consistency and relevance. The resulting dataset, reviewed for coherence and factual accuracy, provides a robust tool for evaluating subjective reasoning in real-world CQA applications.
Stage III: Rigorous Quality Assurance: All chart-question-answer triplets undergo manual review by three annotators experienced in multimodal dataset curation. Evaluations focus on accuracy, clarity, and contextual relevance, with ambiguous or inconsistent triplets revised or excluded. This meticulous review ensures the dataset's alignment with subjective reasoning standards, establishing its reliability for real-world applications.

For example, Qwen2-VL improves its Avg. GPT-4o score from 47.89 (Instruction-following-based) to 56.10 with ChartLLM, while IXC-2.5 increases from 43.31 to 51.88, showcasing significant gains in subjective reasoning. OCR-enhanced methods benefit from comprehensive textual extraction but often introduce redundancy and noise, which can limit their performance in tasks requiring holistic reasoning. COT-based methods enhance reasoning through step-by-step processes, leading to moderate gains. However, their reliance on decomposition strategies makes them less effective in tasks demanding a more integrated understanding of chart content. These results emphasize the robustness and adaptability of ChartLLM-based methods in addressing diverse reasoning challenges.
